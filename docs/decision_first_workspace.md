# Decision-First Workspace

## Core User Actions

The key actions users take today are: **search, send, receive, and decide**. They also contribute, but that is out of scope for now.

Most workplace communication tools are optimized around the first three actions. We believe the real value sits in the fourth: **decision-making**.

## Why AI in Slack Is Not Enough

Simply adding AI agents to Slack is not enough. Every platform can do that.

We are aiming to shift the focus of the work communication suite from **conversations** (send and receive) to **decisions**.

## What It Takes to Make a Decision

A good decision requires **expertise + context**.

Expertise is not just static memory. It is the ability to mix domains effortlessly, recognize non-obvious patterns, and make judgment calls shaped by experience. It includes risk assessment, moral reasoning, and abstract creativity. In many cases, this still requires a human brain.

AI often lacks the ability to make a true judgment call, take principled risks, act morally, or think with genuine abstract creativity.

Humans, on the other hand, have limited focus, imperfect memory, and finite energy. They get distracted. They forget. They need sleep.

So what happens if we combine two operators into a **superstar decision-making unit**?

- **Human:** judgment, taste, accountability, ethics, creativity
- **AI:** recall, synthesis, context gathering, persistence, speed

## Goal

Our goal is to give people a **progressive-disclosure decision-making suite** that removes the burden of gathering information.

It should reduce the effort spent on:
- search
- sending
- receiving
- resolving context gaps

The system should autonomously close as many information gaps as possible before a human is asked to decide.

## Example Persona

**Konrad, Engineering Manager**

Konrad makes **30 to 50 decisions a day**.
Each decision takes roughly **10 minutes**.
Only **3 of those decisions are truly critical**.

He struggles with:
- constant distractions
- incomplete or sparse context
- fragmented information spread across tools

The company may lose a significant amount of money if he makes a bad call on those 3 critical decisions.

That mental and cognitive load also reduces his ability to make higher-value creative contributions such as:
- coding
- architecture design
- strategic thinking

## What Konrad Actually Needs

Konrad needs one place where all relevant context comes together.

One place where:
- simple decisions can be made on the spot
- complex problems are easy to digest and resolve
- making a decision automatically orchestrates the next actions that push work forward

## The Solution

Instead of defining the problem, searching across dozens of apps, messaging colleagues, and responding to other people who are stuck in the same loop, Konrad opens a single page.

On that page, he sees **decisions to be made**, ranked by priority using frameworks such as **ICE** or the **Eisenhower Matrix**.

Each decision includes connected context with **progressive disclosure**:
1. a high-level summary
2. more detailed supporting context
3. messages, links, and files
4. organizational tracing and source history at the end

He chooses how much detail he needs before deciding.

Every decision can be traced back to the exact source context available at that moment.

## Reducing Friction at the Moment of Decision

To remove friction, every decision component includes **recommended actions**.

Because many workplace decisions are operationally binary, the interface can present clear actions such as **Yes** or **No**, with optional comments.

Those decisions can trigger agents to:
- push work forward automatically
- delegate or reassign with a reasoning message
- investigate further by collecting more context
- run deeper research using user-provided sources
- reschedule with a comment and reminder
- place follow-ups directly into the calendar or workflow

## Product Principle

The product should not merely help people communicate.
It should help them **decide with confidence**.

Communication is the input.
Decision is the output.
Progress is what happens next.
